# Methods

**Project:** Talent Portability Research — Does paying for talent work?
**Type:** Synthesis piece (not new empirical research)
**Published by:** The Mallory Group

---

## What this document covers

This note describes the approach used to produce the talent portability synthesis. Because this project does not conduct new data collection or statistical analysis, the methodology is primarily a source evaluation and synthesis protocol.

---

## What this project is

A structured synthesis of seven sources (six peer-reviewed and one industry research) examining whether paying above-market compensation drives measurably better performance outcomes. Two TMG sports research projects, the NHL Free Agency Research and the NBA Supermax Research, provide structural illustration through publicly documented empirical findings. The sports evidence is used as a lens, not as proof.

---

## What this project is not

- New empirical research. No new data was collected.
- A meta-analysis. The sources are not pooled statistically.
- A causal claim. Correlation between pay and performance is documented; causation is not asserted.
- A comprehensive literature review. Seven sources were selected for their direct relevance to the talent portability question. The selection is not exhaustive.

---

## Source selection criteria

Sources were included if they met at least one of the following:

1. Direct empirical evidence on the relationship between compensation and individual or team performance in a context where both variables are measurable.
2. Established theoretical framework used in the academic literature to explain compensation structure or talent mobility.
3. Applied evidence from a specific domain with unusually clean performance measurement (e.g., financial advisors, professional athletes, equity analysts).

Sources were excluded if they were opinion pieces, lacked methodological documentation, or relied on surveys measuring beliefs rather than behaviors or outcomes.

---

## Source review protocol

For each source:

1. Read the original source (journal article, report, or publicly available abstract where full text was paywalled).
2. Assess the methodology: sample size, data quality, identification strategy, and whether the finding is correlational or causal.
3. Check whether the finding has been replicated, revised, or contradicted in subsequent literature (where searchable).
4. Document the limitations specific to each source.
5. Assign a qualitative weight: high, medium-high, or medium.
6. Map the finding to the sports evidence where applicable.

## Weighting rationale

| Source | Weight | Primary reason |
|---|---|---|
| Groysberg et al. (2008) | High | Large N, clean methodology, widely replicated direction |
| Hoisl and Mariani (2017) | High | Same domain as TMG NBA research; granular performance data |
| Topel (1991) | High | Foundational empirical work; well-replicated direction |
| Lazear and Rosen (1981) | High | Foundational theoretical work; empirical tests of tournament predictions are mixed at extremes |
| Lucifora and Simmons (2003) | Medium-high | Soccer data limitations acknowledged; direction replicated in other sports wage studies |
| Berk and Green (2004) | Medium | Theoretical model with indirect empirical support |
| Cerulli Associates (2022) | Medium | Proprietary methodology; directionally consistent; only industry source |

## Sports evidence methodology

The sports findings referenced in this synthesis are drawn from two separate TMG research projects. Neither project is re-analyzed here. The findings are cited as documented outputs of those projects.

- **NHL Free Agency Research:** Nine seasons of NHL UFA data (2017-2025), 1,648 player moves, 284 team-season outcomes. Headline finding: mean reversion slope of approximately -0.42 (p < 0.001). Full methods at the [NHL project repository](https://github.com/rmallorybpc/nhl-free-agency-research/blob/main/METHODS.md).
- **NBA Supermax Research:** Analysis of NBA supermax contract holders and team-level outcomes. Full methods at the [NBA project repository](https://github.com/rmallorybpc/nba-research-project).

---

## Limitations of this synthesis

1. **Sample coverage:** The seven sources cover equity analysts, fund managers, professional athletes, and financial advisors. This does not include technology workers, physicians, lawyers, engineers, or most service-sector roles.

2. **Publication bias:** The peer-reviewed literature may be more likely to publish significant findings. The null-finding sports results provide some counterweight, but the overall evidence base may overstate the detectability of portability effects.

3. **Selection of sources:** Seven sources cannot represent the full literature on compensation and performance. The selection prioritizes direct relevance to the portability question over exhaustiveness.

4. **Analogy limits:** The sports-to-business analogy is productive but has structural limits. Sports performance is measured continuously, publicly, and granularly. Business performance is typically measured annually, privately, and partially.

5. **Theoretical sources:** Two sources (Lazear-Rosen and Berk-Green) are theoretical models. Their inclusion is justified by their central role in the academic framing of these questions, but they do not provide direct empirical evidence.
