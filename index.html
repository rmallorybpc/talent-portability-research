Talent Portability Research

Does paying for talent work?

A synthesis of seven sources examining whether above-market compensation drives measurably better performance outcomes, using sports analytics findings as a structuring lens.

The short answer

Paying more for talent is positively correlated with outcomes at the high end of the talent distribution, but the relationship is neither linear nor universal. The sports evidence shows that large financial commitments to top performers produce meaningful gains only when those performers can actually carry output across contexts. Most cannot. The business research confirms the same pattern: the returns to premium compensation are real, concentrated at the very top of the skill distribution, and frequently overestimated by the organizations doing the paying.

This synthesis does not argue that paying for talent is always wrong. It argues that the decision deserves far more precision than most organizations apply to it.

The sports findings

Two TMG sports research projects provide the structural frame for this synthesis.

The NHL Free Agency Research found that offseason UFA spending does not predict season-over-season performance change after controlling for where a team started. The dominant signal is mean reversion: strong prior-year finishers regress, weak ones improve, and what a team spent in free agency adds little explanatory power. The mean-reversion slope is approximately -0.42 across all model specifications (p < 0.001).

The NBA Supermax Research examined whether supermax-level contracts, the highest compensation tier in professional basketball, translate into proportionally better team outcomes. The evidence is mixed. Supermax players outperform non-supermax peers on average, but the premium paid is not recouped at the team-wins level once roster construction, injuries, and contextual fit are held constant.

Both findings converge on the same structural claim: paying more does not reliably buy more wins, even in domains where performance is precisely measured and talent is the explicit product.

The portability spectrum

The business research literature introduces a concept that reframes the sports findings: talent portability. Not all high performers carry their performance across contexts. Skills that are context-specific, relationship-dependent, or structurally embedded in a team tend to degrade when the individual moves. Skills that are general, codifiable, or tied to individual cognitive capacity tend to transfer.

This creates a spectrum. At one end: the superstar surgeon, the elite quantitative analyst, the generative AI researcher. Their skills travel. At the other end: the star sales executive whose relationships are with their employer's brand, the top-performing manager whose team made them look good, the VP whose success came from organizational positioning. Their skills stay behind when they leave.

The organizations that overpay for talent tend to underprice this distinction.

What this piece can and cannot claim

This is a synthesis, not new empirical research. Every finding cited here comes from one of the seven sources in the citations annex. The sports data adds structural illustration, not causal proof. The framing is interpretive and the limits of each source are documented in the audit.

The strongest claims are those that appear across multiple independent sources and align with the sports evidence. The weakest are those from single studies with narrow samples. See the audit page for a full accounting.
