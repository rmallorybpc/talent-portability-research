Talent Portability Research

Audit

What this piece can claim, what it cannot, where the analogy weakens, and which sources carry the most weight.

What this synthesis can claim

The following claims are supported by multiple independent sources and are unlikely to be artifacts of any single study's methodology or sample.

  • Premium compensation does not proportionally increase team-level output. This holds across NHL free agency, NBA supermax contracts, and the Lucifora-Simmons soccer wage study.
  • A meaningful share of high-performer output is context-specific and does not transfer on hire. The Groysberg equity analyst study and the Hoisl-Mariani NBA mobility study both document this directly. Topel's wage tenure work provides the foundational economic mechanism.
  • The labor market for top talent is competitive enough that stars capture most of their economic value through wages. The Berk-Green mutual fund manager model demonstrates this theoretically; the Cerulli advisor study provides applied evidence in a different domain.
  • Performance returns to pay are nonlinear and flatten at the very top of the distribution. Supported by Lucifora-Simmons directly and consistent with the tournament theory framework of Lazear-Rosen.

What this synthesis cannot claim

The following claims are not supported by this synthesis and would require additional evidence to assert.

  • That organizations should not pay above-market wages for top talent. This synthesis documents diminishing and context-dependent returns, not zero returns. The claim is about calibration, not prohibition.
  • That talent portability is low in all domains. The Cerulli advisor study shows approximately 85% portability at three years for financial advisors. Some domains show high portability. This synthesis identifies the conditions that predict high versus low portability; it does not claim universally low portability.
  • That the sports evidence is a causal proof of business patterns. The sports data provides a structuring analogy and illustrative evidence. It is not a causal chain. The business outcomes of paying above-market wages to executives or professionals are not directly measured in the TMG sports datasets.
  • That mean reversion in sports is the same phenomenon as performance decline after a hire. They may share mechanisms, but mean reversion in the NHL dataset is a team-level statistical phenomenon. Post-hire performance decline in the Groysberg and Hoisl-Mariani studies is an individual-level behavioral phenomenon. The analogy is illustrative, not mechanistic.

Where the analogy weakens

The sports-to-business analogy is productive but has real limits.

Performance measurement

Sports performance is measured continuously, publicly, and granularly. Points percentage, goals, assists, win shares, and plus-minus are computed for every game. Business performance for most roles is measured annually, privately, partially, and with significant managerial discretion. This makes the sports evidence unusually clean and the business application less direct.

Market structure

NHL and NBA salary structures are regulated. Salary caps constrain the total expenditure and the maximum individual contract. Most business labor markets have neither. The NHL and NBA findings may understate or overstate what would be observed in uncapped markets.

Team interdependence

Hockey and basketball are highly interdependent team sports. The value of any individual player is partially determined by their teammates. The interdependence structure of most business roles is different and harder to measure. Roles where output is more individual (solo contributor roles, quantitative specialists) may show higher portability than the sports evidence suggests.

Sample coverage

The seven sources cover equity analysts, fund managers, professional athletes, financial advisors, and theoretical labor markets. This sample does not include technology workers, physicians, lawyers, engineers, or most service-sector roles. Generalizing to those domains requires additional evidence not present in this synthesis.

Source weight

The sources are not equally strong. Below is a plain-language quality assessment.

Source Type Weight Notes
Groysberg et al. (2008) Peer-reviewed High Large N, clean methodology, widely replicated direction
Hoisl and Mariani (2017) Peer-reviewed High Same domain as TMG NBA research; granular performance data
Topel (1991) Peer-reviewed High Foundational empirical work; methodology is standard
Lazear and Rosen (1981) Peer-reviewed (theoretical) High Foundational theory; empirical tests are mixed at very top
Lucifora and Simmons (2003) Peer-reviewed Medium-high Soccer data has credit-assignment limitations; direction replicated
Berk and Green (2004) Peer-reviewed (theoretical) Medium Theoretical model; strong indirect empirical support
Cerulli Associates (2022) Industry research Medium Proprietary methodology; directionally consistent; positive portability case

Methodology note

This synthesis did not conduct new data collection or statistical analysis. Source evaluation followed a structured review protocol: reading the original source, assessing the methodology, checking whether the finding has been replicated or contradicted in subsequent literature, and documenting limitations. See METHODS.md for the full protocol.
