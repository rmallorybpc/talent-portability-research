Talent Portability Research

Implications

What this research means for HR, compensation design, and executive decision making.

The central implication

The research says: before paying a premium for talent, determine what share of that talent will actually arrive.

Every external hire is a portability bet. Some of that person's prior output came from their individual skill. Some came from their colleagues, their organizational systems, their relationships, their brand, and the competitive positioning of their prior employer. The first kind travels with them. The second kind does not.

Organizations that treat external hires as pure additions of the candidate's prior-employer output will systematically overpay and be systematically disappointed. The research is consistent enough on this point that the burden of proof should shift: before paying above-market rates for a known performer, the question is not "were they successful before?" but "what fraction of that success was theirs to carry?"

For HR and talent acquisition

Assess portability before setting the offer

The Groysberg framework suggests a practical diagnostic: how much of this person's current output depends on assets they own versus assets they borrow from their employer? Relationships that are theirs, skills that are theirs, reputation that is theirs. These transfer. Relationships that belong to the firm, systems that the firm built, a brand that the firm created. These stay behind.

Weight internal development more heavily in the analysis

Topel's wage tenure findings show that firm-specific human capital is large. An employee who has been with an organization for five years has likely accumulated substantial context-specific value. Replacing that person with an external hire at a premium price recovers only the portable fraction of the departing employee's capability. Development investments that retain people tend to show better returns than external acquisitions at the same capability level.

Expect a first-year dip

The Hoisl-Mariani finding that NBA players experience a post-move performance decline suggests a structural adjustment period. The same pattern is plausible for business hires. Performance evaluations in year one of tenure should be interpreted in light of the context-recalibration effect. Judging a new hire against their prior-employer output in their first twelve months is likely to produce incorrect conclusions about their capability.

For compensation design

The nonlinearity problem

The Lucifora-Simmons finding that individual performance returns flatten at the top of the pay distribution has a direct compensation design implication: above a certain level, incremental pay does not buy incremental output. The marginal cost of moving from the 90th percentile of compensation to the 99th percentile is substantial; the marginal output gain is not proportional. Organizations designing compensation structures for high-level roles should model this nonlinearity explicitly rather than assuming linear returns.

The tournament effect is real but may be over-relied upon

Lazear-Rosen tournament theory correctly identifies that large prizes at the top generate incentive effects throughout the organization. But the effect operates through the expectation of winning, not through the compensation of current winners. Once a tournament prize is established, increasing it further produces diminishing additional incentive. Very large executive pay differentials are sometimes justified on tournament grounds, but the marginal incentive contribution of increases at the very top of already large differentials is small.

Retention versus acquisition

The Berk-Green mechanism and the Topel finding together suggest that retention of existing top performers is likely to produce better returns than acquisition of equivalent performers at market rates. The existing employee's firm-specific capital is already accumulated. The market wage for an external hire will reflect only their portable value, which is a subset of their total value at your organization if they stay. Spending equivalent dollars on retention rather than acquisition is systematically underweighted in most talent investment models.

For executive decision making

The franchise player problem

Sports organizations face a version of this problem directly: whether to pay a franchise player at supermax levels when the research suggests team-level returns do not match individual-level performance. The business equivalent is the star executive, the top rainmaker, the irreplaceable engineer. The decision calculus is the same: is the premium above their market wage recovering firm-specific capital, or is it paying for output that would occur at market rate?

Mean reversion as a signal

The NHL finding that prior-year performance is the strongest predictor of next-year change, with a mean reversion coefficient of approximately -0.42, has a business parallel. Exceptional performance in a prior role should generate some skepticism about whether it is repeatable in a new context. Outstanding prior performance is a valid signal of capability but a less valid signal of future performance in a different environment. Executive searches that weight prior-role outcomes too heavily, without accounting for the context-dependence of those outcomes, will produce a systematic selection error.

Fewer, better-calibrated premium bets

The consistent finding across all seven sources is that the returns to premium compensation are real but concentrated, context-dependent, and nonlinear. The practical implication is not to stop paying for talent. It is to pay for talent with more precision: fewer premium bets, better analysis of portability before those bets are placed, and more investment in the organizational context that generates top performance rather than the purchase of performance from outside.

A closing note

These implications are drawn from the research without prescribing a specific service, tool, or methodology. The point is not to arrive at a universal rule about compensation. It is to introduce more precision into a decision that most organizations make with less analytical rigor than the stakes warrant.

The sports world has been forced into precision by public performance data and salary cap constraints. The business world typically has neither. But the analytical framework that makes sports compensation decisions tractable applies equally to business contexts where the data is harder to find but the questions are the same.
