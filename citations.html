Talent Portability Research

Citations

Full bibliography for the synthesis. Seven sources with working links, review notes, and source type. Six peer-reviewed, one industry research.

How to read this annex

Each entry includes the full citation, the source type, a direct link to the source or its abstract, and a brief review note explaining why the source is included and how it was evaluated. Where a source is behind a paywall, a link to the abstract or journal page is provided. The business research page contains a full treatment of each source's findings and their mapping to the sports evidence.

Citation 1 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Superstar extinction

Groysberg, B., Lee, L.-E., and Nanda, A. (2008). American Economic Review, 98(3), 1285-1289. doi:10.1257/aer.98.3.1289

Review note

Published in the American Economic Review. Based on a dataset of 1,052 star equity analysts tracked across employer changes. The finding that performance declines on average after a move, with the sharpest decline for analysts in highly interdependent roles, is central to the portability framework. This is the highest-weight source in the synthesis for the portability-is-context-dependent claim. The longer companion paper ("The Risky Business of Hiring Stars," Harvard Business Review, 2004) provides additional qualitative context.

Citation 2 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Mutual fund flows and performance in rational markets

Berk, J. B. and Green, R. C. (2004). Journal of Finance, 59(3), 1269-1295. doi:10.1111/j.1540-6261.2004.00637.x

Review note

Published in the Journal of Finance. This is a theoretical model paper. The core prediction, that skilled managers capture their value through wages in a competitive market, has been empirically supported by subsequent mutual fund research. The mechanism is used in this synthesis to explain why acquiring top talent at market rate may leave limited surplus for the acquiring organization. Assigned medium weight as a theoretical rather than empirical source.

Citation 3 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Human capital portability and individual performance: evidence from professional basketball

Hoisl, K. and Mariani, M. (2017). Strategic Management Journal, 38(12), 2349-2375. doi:10.1002/smj.2617

Review note

Published in the Strategic Management Journal. This source is directly relevant to the NBA supermax research and uses the same domain. The clean performance data in professional basketball makes this a high-quality natural experiment. The distinction between general and specific human capital in the portability context is well-developed here and maps directly to the portability spectrum framing in this synthesis. Assigned high weight.

Citation 4 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Rank-order tournaments as optimum labor contracts

Lazear, E. P. and Rosen, S. (1981). Journal of Political Economy, 89(5), 841-864. doi:10.1086/261153

Review note

Published in the Journal of Political Economy. This is a foundational theoretical paper that provides the economic justification for large compensation differentials at senior levels. It is included here to establish the theoretical baseline: tournament theory is a valid partial explanation for premium executive pay. The synthesis uses the tournament framework to note that the incentive function is diffuse, not concentrated in current top earners, which helps explain why team-level returns in sports do not scale with the salary of the top player.

Citation 5 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Superstar effects in sport: evidence from Italian soccer

Lucifora, C. and Simmons, R. (2003). Journal of Labor Economics, 21(2), 443-459. doi:10.1086/380413

Review note

Published in the Journal of Labor Economics. The soccer performance data has credit-assignment limitations compared to basketball (goals are rare, team effects are large), but the wage dataset is unusually complete. The nonlinearity finding at the top of the pay distribution is the key contribution for this synthesis. Assigned medium-high weight; the direction is consistent with the sports findings and replicated in baseball wage studies, but the soccer-specific methodology has acknowledged limitations.

Citation 6 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Specific capital, mobility, and wages: wages rise with job seniority

Topel, R. (1991). Journal of Political Economy, 99(1), 145-176. doi:10.1086/261736

Review note

Published in the Journal of Political Economy. This is a landmark empirical paper using matched employer-employee data. The finding that wages rise with tenure beyond what general skill accumulation can explain is the empirical foundation for the claim that firm-specific human capital is economically large. Subsequent research with better data has debated the magnitude of the tenure premium but confirmed the direction. Assigned high weight as the foundational empirical work on firm-specific capital.

Citation 7 of 7  ·  Industry research

U.S. Advisor Metrics 2022

Cerulli Associates (2022). The Cerulli Report: U.S. Advisor Metrics. cerulli.com/research/us-advisor-metrics

Review note

Industry research from Cerulli Associates, a proprietary financial services research firm. The methodology is not publicly disclosed and the underlying data is not available for independent replication. This source is included because the financial advisory context is unusually clean for measuring talent portability (AUM is a directly observable output metric) and because it provides a positive portability case that balances the more negative portability evidence from the peer-reviewed sources. Directional consistency with the peer-reviewed literature is the primary basis for inclusion. Assigned medium weight.

Dai, H., Milkman, K. L., and Riis, J. (2014). The fresh start effect: temporal landmarks motivate aspirational behavior. Management Science, 60(10), 2563-2582. doi:10.1287/mnsc.2014.1901
