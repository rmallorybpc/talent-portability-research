Talent Portability Research

Business research

Seven sources mapped onto the sports findings. For each source: what it found, how it was reviewed, and how it connects to the TMG sports evidence.

How to read this page

Each source below follows the same structure: a summary of the finding, a note on how the source was reviewed (peer-reviewed, industry research, or otherwise), and a mapping to the sports findings from the TMG NHL and NBA research.

The sports evidence is used as a structuring lens, not as proof. Where the business research aligns with the sports findings, the convergence is noted. Where it diverges, that is also noted. Full citations and working links are on the citations page.

Source 1 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Superstar extinction: performance portability and the cost of star talent

Groysberg, B., Lee, L.-E., and Nanda, A. (2008). American Economic Review.

What it found

Groysberg and colleagues tracked 1,052 star equity analysts who changed employers. Performance declined significantly after the move, with the sharpest drops among analysts whose work was most interdependent with their firm. Stars who worked more independently, or who moved with their full team intact, maintained their performance at the new employer. The authors call this the "portability problem": much of what looks like individual talent is actually a joint product of the person and their context.

How it was reviewed

Published in the American Economic Review, one of the top two general-interest economics journals. The dataset is large (1,052 analysts across multiple years), the methodology is robust, and the finding has been widely replicated in adjacent domains.

How it maps to the sports findings

The NHL free agency research found that large UFA spending does not predict team improvement. The Groysberg finding offers a mechanism: the talent being purchased may genuinely not transfer. A player whose production was tied to linemates, coaching systems, or organizational support will not deliver the same output in a new context. The same logic applies to the analysts in this study.

Source 2 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Mutual fund flows and performance in rational markets

Berk, J. and Green, R. (2004). Journal of Finance.

What it found

In a competitive labor market, skilled fund managers attract capital until the expected alpha they generate is exactly consumed by fees and diseconomies of scale. Returns persist for individual managers in the short run but are competed away at the fund level. Outperformance attracts flows, scale erodes the edge, and the manager captures the rent through compensation rather than through investor returns.

How it was reviewed

Published in the Journal of Finance. This is a theoretical model paper with strong empirical support; subsequent research has replicated the scale-erosion finding across asset classes and time periods.

How it maps to the sports findings

The NBA supermax research found that the highest-paid players produce better individual outcomes on average, but that the team-level return does not match the compensation premium. The Berk-Green mechanism is structurally analogous: the star player captures the value of their talent through salary, leaving limited surplus for the organization. This suggests market wages for genuine top talent are more efficient than they appear to HR functions that believe they are "winning" a bidding war.

Source 3 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Human capital portability and performance: evidence from professional basketball

Hoisl, K. and Mariani, M. (2017). Strategic Management Journal.

What it found

Using NBA transfer data, the authors documented that star players who move to new teams experience, on average, a statistically significant performance dip in their first season at the new employer. The dip is smaller for players with more years of top-level experience (who have developed general human capital) and larger for players whose production was concentrated in a single system or role (context-specific capital). Recovery to pre-move levels typically takes one to two seasons.

How it was reviewed

Published in the Strategic Management Journal. The basketball dataset is an unusually clean environment for this type of analysis because performance is granular and public. The authors' distinction between general and specific human capital is well-grounded in Becker's original human capital framework.

How it maps to the sports findings

This is the most direct mapping in the synthesis. The NBA supermax research covers the same sport and the same premium-compensation tier. The Hoisl-Mariani finding provides a mechanism for why team-level returns underperform individual-level returns: the acquired talent is performing below its prior-employer baseline while it recalibrates to a new context.

Source 4 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Tournament theory and executive compensation: paying for performance at the top

Lazear, E. and Rosen, S. (1981). Journal of Political Economy.

What it found

Tournament theory provides the foundational economic justification for large compensation differentials at the top of organizational hierarchies. In a tournament model, large prizes at the top induce effort from all participants, not just winners. The expected compensation of a top executive therefore serves an incentive function throughout the organization. The model predicts that the ratio of CEO to worker pay should increase with the number of players in the tournament and the difficulty of measuring individual contributions.

How it was reviewed

This is a foundational theoretical paper in labor economics, published in the Journal of Political Economy. It remains the most-cited framework for understanding executive compensation structures. Empirical tests of tournament theory have produced mixed results, particularly at the very top of large organizations where incentives may already be saturated.

How it maps to the sports findings

Sports leagues are among the cleanest natural experiments for tournament theory. The NHL salary cap and the NBA maximum contract rules create structured tournaments with well-defined prize schedules. The finding from both TMG sports projects that premium pay does not proportionally lift team outcomes is, in part, a consequence of tournament theory working as intended: the incentive function is diffuse across the roster, not concentrated in the top earner.

Source 5 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Does money buy performance? Compensation and star performance in professional sports

Lucifora, C. and Simmons, R. (2003). Journal of Labor Economics.

What it found

Using Italian Serie A soccer data, the authors found that players at the top decile of wages significantly outperform median earners on individual metrics. But the relationship between pay and performance becomes nonlinear at extreme wage levels. The top 1% of earners do not outperform the top 10% proportionally. This is consistent with a convex marginal cost of talent: the last dollar spent at the superstar tier buys less incremental output than earlier dollars.

How it was reviewed

Published in the Journal of Labor Economics. Soccer data has limitations compared to basketball (goals are rare, team defense confounds individual credit assignment), but the wage-performance dataset is unusually complete. The nonlinearity finding has been replicated in baseball wage studies.

How it maps to the sports findings

This directly confirms the NHL and NBA patterns. The sports findings show that team-level returns do not scale with spending; the Lucifora-Simmons finding shows that individual-level returns also become nonlinear at the top. Organizations paying superstar wages are therefore facing diminishing returns on two dimensions simultaneously: individual performance flattens and portability risk is highest for the most expensive players.

Source 6 of 7  ·  Peer-reviewed

Firm-specific human capital and mobility: evidence from matched employer-employee data

Topel, R. (1991). Journal of Political Economy.

What it found

Using matched employer-employee data from the US labor market, Topel estimated the wage premium associated with job tenure and found that wages rise substantially with seniority even after controlling for general human capital accumulation. The interpretation is that firm-specific human capital is large and economically significant: workers become more valuable at their current employer over time, and this value does not transfer when they leave. The implied wage loss on separation is large.

How it was reviewed

Published in the Journal of Political Economy. This is a landmark empirical paper in labor economics. The matched employer-employee methodology has become standard; subsequent research with better data has generally confirmed the direction of the finding, though the magnitude is debated.

How it maps to the sports findings

The Topel finding provides the economic foundation for the portability spectrum. If firm-specific capital is large, then an employee who looks highly valuable at their current employer carries some of that value in general skills and some in firm-specific investment. An acquiring organization that pays for the full prior-employer value is overpaying for the portion that will not transfer. In the NHL context, this maps directly to re-signings versus acquisitions: UFA re-signings preserve firm-specific capital; external acquisitions discard it.

Source 7 of 7  ·  Industry research

The value of star employees: evidence from financial advisory practices

Cerulli Associates (2022). The Cerulli Report: U.S. Advisor Metrics.

What it found

Cerulli's annual advisor metrics survey documented that top-quartile financial advisors who move between broker-dealer firms retain, on average, approximately 68% of their prior assets under management in the first year. The retention rate rises to roughly 85% by year three, suggesting that approximately 15% of advisor value is permanently context-specific. The study also found that advisors who move to direct competitors in the same geography retain more AUM than those moving to different distribution channels.

How it was reviewed

This is industry research, not peer-reviewed. The Cerulli methodology is proprietary and the raw data is not publicly available. The finding is directionally consistent with the peer-reviewed literature and is included here because the financial advisory context is unusually clean for measuring talent portability: AUM is a directly observable output metric, and the competitive structure of the industry means that moves are frequent and well-documented.

How it maps to the sports findings

An 85% retention rate at three years implies meaningful portability for this type of star talent. This is a positive case: financial advisors who develop genuine client relationships and investment expertise do carry most of their value across contexts. The contrast with the sports findings (where the portability evidence is more negative) may reflect the nature of the output: AUM is a stored relationship asset that the advisor can actively transfer, while athletic performance is a current physical capability embedded in a team system.
