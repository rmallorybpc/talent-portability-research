Talent Portability Research

What the business research says

Seven sources addressing the same question asked by The Mallory Group sports studies.

How to read this page

Each source includes four parts: citation and link, what was found, how it maps to sports findings, and mapping strength. The honest synthesis answer: paying for outside talent works sometimes. It depends on whether the asset transfers.

Why paying for outside talent often fails

Groysberg (2010): star portability

Chasing Stars tracks more than 1,000 star Wall Street analysts at 78 banks. Analysts who switched firms declined, often immediately and durably. Exceptions: team moves, moves to higher-capability firms, and female stars. Two TMG sports studies map strongly to this mechanism at player level.

Bidwell (2011): the external hire premium

Paying more to get less finds external hires are paid around 18 percent more than internal promotions, with lower evaluations in the first two years and higher exits. NHL and NFL team-level findings and NHL contract-level findings show parallel buyer-side economics.

Roll (1986): the hubris hypothesis

The hubris hypothesis of corporate takeovers explains recurring overpayment via overconfidence in buyer valuation. This is mechanism-level support for repeated overpayment behavior seen in sports free-agency markets.

Boivie, Gee, Gentry, and Graffin (2025): experience does not fix the error

Do boards learn to hire? studies S&P 1500 firms from 1999 to 2020 and finds prior CEO-selection experience predicts slightly worse subsequent CEO outcomes. The paper describes this as suggestive of superstitious learning.

When talent does transfer

Berry-Stolzle and Eckles (2019)

It is about building a book of business finds property-liability insurance policy renewal averages 89 percent and is strongly tied to salesperson output and compensation. This is a portability counterpoint.

Gurun, Stoffman, and Yonker (2021)

Unlocking clients uses Protocol for Broker Recruiting variation and finds about 40 percent of client assets follow advisors when they move. This maps on portability, not direct post-move performance.

Practitioner-side numbers

Cerulli reports transition asset loss for advisor moves (about 22 percent for broker-dealer moves, about 18 percent for independent moves). This supports practitioner context but remains weaker evidence than peer-reviewed studies.

What the seven sources synthesize to

  1. Buyer-side overpayment is persistent, and experience does not reliably correct it.
  2. Star performance is contextual more often than portable.
  3. Transfer improves when the value-creating asset is portable.

The sports findings and business research are pointing the same direction. That convergence is the synthesis.

Further context

Bertrand and Mullainathan (2001) appears as a footnote citation in this project. It provides adjacent evidence on CEO pay sensitivity to luck and governance, but it does not carry analytical weight on portability itself.
