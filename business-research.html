Talent Portability Research

What the business research says

Seven sources on whether paying for outside talent delivers expected performance.

How to read this page

Each source has four parts: the citation and link, what the source found, how it maps to TMG sports studies, and an honest read on mapping strength. Sources are grouped into failures of outside-talent buying, portability counterpoints, and practitioner-side context.

Why paying for outside talent often fails

Groysberg (2010): star portability

Chasing Stars: The Myth of Talent and the Portability of Performance finds star analysts decline after moving firms, with exceptions for team moves and moves to stronger firms. Two TMG studies mirror this at player level: NBA movers decline versus stayers, and NHL same-team contracts outperform new-team contracts per dollar. Strength: strong.

Bidwell (2011): the external hire premium

Paying more to get less finds external hires are paid around 18 percent more, then score lower in performance evaluations for two years and exit more often. NHL, NFL, and NHL Play for Contract show similar buyer-side dynamics in sports. Strength: strong.

Roll (1986): the hubris hypothesis

The hubris hypothesis of corporate takeovers explains why bidders overpay despite weak returns. This maps to a mechanism behind repeated free-agency overpayment, rather than directly to sports outcomes. Strength: medium.

Boivie et al. (2025): experience does not correct hiring errors

Do boards learn to hire? finds more board CEO-hiring experience predicts slightly worse later CEO performance, not better. The paper characterizes this as suggestive of superstitious learning. This strengthens the claim that buyer-side errors persist over time. Strength: strong on mechanism.

When talent does transfer

Berry-Stolzle and Eckles (2019)

It is about building a book of business finds property-liability insurance books renew at 89 percent on average. This is a portability counterpoint: when the asset is a portable relationship book, transfer is stronger. Strength: medium as counterpoint.

Gurun, Stoffman, and Yonker (2021)

Unlocking clients finds about 40 percent of client assets follow advisors when they move, using Protocol for Broker Recruiting variation. This maps to portability, not direct performance outcome. Strength: medium-strong.

Practitioner-side numbers

Cerulli Associates reports advisors switching broker-dealers lose about 22 percent of assets during transition, and moves to independent models lose about 18 percent. These are useful context numbers but are weaker as evidence because methods are proprietary and not peer-reviewed.

What the seven sources synthesize to

  1. Buyers overpay and experience does not reliably correct the error.
  2. Star performance is often contextual more than portable.
  3. Transfer happens when the value-creating asset is portable.

The sports findings and business research point in the same direction. That convergence is the synthesis.
